Top 25 must-knows

Skim these now. You'll see them again at the end of every section as spaced review. By Sunday they'll be reflexive.

  1. Foregut = celiac (T12) → portal/splenic v. Midgut = SMA (L1) → SMV. Hindgut = IMA (L3) → IMV.
  2. Foregut pain = epigastric (T5–T9). Midgut = periumbilical (T10–T11). Hindgut = suprapubic (L1–L2).
  3. Hindgut parasymp = pelvic splanchnics (S2–S4). Not vagus.
  4. Caudate lobe drains direct into IVC → hypertrophies in Budd-Chiari.
  5. Hepatoduodenal lig contains portal triad (bile duct, hepatic a. proper, portal v.). Pringle maneuver.
  6. Left gonadal/suprarenal v. → left renal v. → IVC. Right → IVC direct.
  7. SAD PUCKER = retroperitoneal: Suprarenals, Aorta/IVC, Duodenum (2–4), Pancreas (not tail), Ureters, Colon (asc/desc), Kidneys, Esophagus, Rectum.
  8. Marginal a. of Drummond connects SMA ↔ IMA. Watershed at splenic flexure.
  9. SMA: anterior to 3rd duodenum (SMA syndrome) + anterior to L renal v. (nutcracker).
  10. McBurney pain → appendix → appendicular a. → ileocolic a. → SMA. Surgeon ligates ileocolic.
  11. Direct hernia = medial to inf. epigastric (Hesselbach's). Indirect = lateral.
  12. Spermatic cord layers: ext. spermatic (ext. oblique) → cremaster (int. oblique) → int. spermatic (transversalis fascia).
  13. Ilioinguinal n. (L1) = anterior scrotum/labia + medial thigh. Cut in appendectomy/hernia repair.
  14. Testis drains to para-aortic nodes. Scrotum drains to superficial inguinal. Don't confuse.
  15. Diaphragm: I 8 (ate) 10 EGGs At 12 → IVC T8, esoph T10, aorta T12.
  16. Dermatomes: T7 = xiphoid, T10 = umbilicus, L1 = inguinal, T8 = subcostal margin.
  17. Inf. epigastric a. enters rectus sheath at arcuate line. Below it: no posterior sheath.
  18. Cystic a. from right hepatic a. (Calot's triangle).
  19. Aorta: 3 unpaired (celiac T12, SMA L1, IMA L3) + 5 paired. Bifurcates L4.
  20. Pancreas tail = intraperitoneal (splenorenal lig). Head/body/uncinate = retroperitoneal.
  21. Liver lobes: right, left, caudate (between IVC + lig venosum), quadrate (between GB + lig teres).
  22. Lumbar plexus to psoas: obturator MEDIAL, femoral LATERAL, genitofemoral on ANTERIOR surface.
  23. Ureter constrictions: UPJ → pelvic brim → UVJ.
  24. Splenectomy via gastrosplenic lig: cuts short gastrics → fundus ischemia.
  25. Greater curvature = R + L gastro-omental aa. Lesser = R + L gastric aa. Conduit survives on R gastro-omental.

Chunk 1 of 7 — Bony framework

The skeleton supporting the abdomen consists of:

  • Lower ribs: 7–10 (false ribs, attach to costal cartilage of rib above), 11–12 (floating ribs, no anterior attachment)
  • Xiphoid process: inferior tip of sternum, sits at T9 vertebral level
  • Costal margin: lower border of rib cage; meets in midline at xiphoid
  • Vertebrae: T12 + L1–L5 form posterior support; L1 vertebral body sits at the transpyloric plane
  • Pelvis: iliac crest (top of ilium), ASIS, iliac tubercle (5cm posterior to ASIS), pubic symphysis, pubic tubercle

Chunk 3 of 7 — Important surface landmarks

Vertical / planes:

  • Median plane: midline; divides into left/right halves
  • Midclavicular lines: L & R, used for the 9 regions
  • Transumbilical plane: through umbilicus (~L3–L4); divides into upper/lower halves for 4 quadrants

Horizontal:

  • Subcostal plane: across lowest costal margin (~L3)
  • Transtubercular plane: between iliac tubercles (~L5)
  • Transpyloric plane: midway xiphoid to umbilicus (~L1) — passes through pylorus, neck of pancreas, fundus of GB

Surface points:

  • Umbilicus: ~T10 dermatome, ~L3–L4 vertebral level
  • McBurney point: 1/3 distance from ASIS to umbilicus → surface marking of appendix base
  • Murphy point: tip of right 9th costal cartilage → fundus of gallbladder

Chunk 6 of 7 — Rectus abdominis & the rectus sheath

Anterior abdominal wall cadaver: linea alba, external oblique, rectus sheath (Michigan BlueLink). Click to enlarge.

Rectus abdominis: paired strap muscle running from pubic crest/symphysis to costal cartilages 5–7 + xiphoid. Has 3 tendinous intersections (creating the "six-pack" appearance).

The rectus sheath is formed by the aponeuroses of the 3 flat muscles. Its composition CHANGES at the arcuate line (~halfway between umbilicus and pubic symphysis):

Above arcuate line
Below arcuate line
Anterior sheath: ext. oblique + ant. half of int. oblique aponeuroses
Anterior sheath: ALL THREE aponeuroses pass anterior to rectus
Posterior sheath: post. half of int. oblique + transversus aponeuroses
NO posterior rectus sheath — only transversalis fascia behind rectus

Linea alba: midline raphe formed by interdigitating fibers of all 3 aponeuroses. Linea semilunaris: lateral border of rectus.

A stab wound below the arcuate line, just lateral to linea alba, perforates rectus → next layer hit is transversalis fascia (NOT posterior rectus sheath — it doesn't exist down there).

Chunk 7 of 7 — Vasculature & nerves of the wall

Arteries:

  • Superior epigastric a. = continuation of internal thoracic a. → enters rectus sheath superiorly through diaphragm
  • Inferior epigastric a. = branch of external iliac a. → enters rectus sheath at the arcuate line
  • These anastomose vertically → basis of TRAM flap (which sacrifices inferior epigastric)
  • Deep circumflex iliac a. (also from external iliac) supplies lateral wall

Cutaneous nerves: from anterior rami of T7–L1 spinal nerves. Subcostal nerve = T12. Iliohypogastric (L1) + ilioinguinal (L1) supply suprapubic + inguinal regions. Each gives anterior + lateral cutaneous branches.

The inferior epigastric a. is the dividing landmark for hernia type. Lateral to it = indirect. Medial to it = direct. Coming up in Theme 2.
Common hernia surface locations: epigastric (linea alba above umbilicus), umbilical (through navel), spigelian (along linea semilunaris below arcuate line), incisional (through old surgical scars), inguinal (above inguinal lig), femoral (below inguinal lig, medial to femoral v.).

Chunk 1 of 7 — The inguinal canal: definition + course

The inguinal canal is an oblique passageway (~4 cm long) through the lower abdominal wall, parallel and just superior to the medial half of the inguinal ligament.

Course: begins at the deep inguinal ring (lateral, opening in transversalis fascia, lateral to inferior epigastric a.) → runs medially → ends at the superficial inguinal ring (medial, opening in external oblique aponeurosis, just superolateral to pubic tubercle).

It carries the spermatic cord (males) or round ligament of the uterus (females), plus the ilioinguinal nerve.

Chunk 3 of 7 — Hesselbach's triangle

A weak spot on the posterior wall of the inguinal canal — site of direct inguinal hernias.

Boundaries (mnemonic: "RIP"):

  • Rectus abdominis (medial border)
  • Inferior epigastric a. (lateral border)
  • Poupart's (inguinal) ligament (inferior border)

The floor (posterior wall) is just transversalis fascia — easily bulges outward in older men with chronic abdominal strain.

Chunk 5 of 7 — Spermatic cord contents (rule of 3s)

Testis + epididymis (head, body, tail) cadaver labeling (Michigan BlueLink). Click to enlarge.

3 arteries:

  • Testicular a. (from abdominal aorta at L2)
  • Cremasteric a. (from inferior epigastric a.)
  • Artery of ductus deferens (from inferior vesical a.)

3 nerves:

  • Genital branch of genitofemoral n. (motor to cremaster, sensory to scrotum)
  • Ilioinguinal n. (technically ON the cord, not in it)
  • Autonomic fibers (sympathetic to ductus deferens)

3 other:

  • Ductus (vas) deferens
  • Pampiniform plexus of veins (drains testicle; left → left renal v.; right → IVC)
  • Lymphatics (to para-aortic nodes)

Chunk 1 of 6 — What IS the peritoneum?

A continuous serous membrane lining the abdominal cavity (parietal peritoneum) and reflecting onto the organs (visceral peritoneum).

Between them is the peritoneal cavity — a potential space containing only a thin film of lubricating fluid.

  • Parietal peritoneum: somatically innervated → sharp, well-localized pain
  • Visceral peritoneum: visceral afferent fibers → dull, poorly-localized referred pain

Chunk 2 of 6 — Intra vs. retroperitoneal (the SAD PUCKER mnemonic)

SAD PUCKER = primarily retroperitoneal organs (sit behind the parietal peritoneum on the posterior wall):

  • Suprarenal glands
  • Aorta & IVC
  • Duodenum (parts 2, 3, 4)
  • Pancreas (except tail)
  • Ureters
  • Colon (ascending & descending only)
  • Kidneys
  • Esophagus (lower)
  • Rectum (lower 2/3)

Intraperitoneal (suspended by mesentery, mobile): stomach, 1st duodenum, jejunum, ileum, cecum + appendix, transverse colon, sigmoid, liver, gallbladder, spleen, tail of pancreas.

Secondarily retroperitoneal: structures that started intraperitoneal but became fixed during development (ascending colon, descending colon, duodenum 2-4, pancreas head/body).

The favorite twist: tail of pancreas is intraperitoneal (it sits in the splenorenal ligament). Head/body/uncinate are retroperitoneal. Same goes for duodenum: only the 1st part is intraperitoneal — parts 2-4 are retroperitoneal.
Predict & Check
Pick the intraperitoneal one: 2nd part of duodenum, ascending colon, transverse colon, kidney, suprarenal gland.
Transverse colon (suspended by transverse mesocolon). All others are retroperitoneal.

Chunk 3 of 6 — Greater sac, lesser sac, omental foramen

The peritoneal cavity has 2 compartments:

  • Greater peritoneal sac: the main space. Subdivided into supracolic (above transverse mesocolon) and infracolic (below). Infracolic has L & R paracolic gutters.
  • Lesser sac (omental bursa): behind the stomach + lesser omentum. Allows the stomach to move freely.

They communicate via the omental (epiploic) foramen of Winslow:

Chunk 6 of 6 — Peritoneal recesses (where fluid pools)

Liver, bare area, coronary lig, hepatorenal recess, subphrenic recess — Block 4 GI lecture diagram. Click to enlarge.

When you have ascites or hemoperitoneum, fluid collects in the lowest peritoneal recesses (clinically important):

  • Subphrenic recess: between liver + diaphragm; common abscess site post-perforation
  • Hepatorenal recess (Morison's pouch): between liver + R kidney; most dependent space when supine → first place fluid collects
  • Paracolic gutters: lateral to ascending + descending colon; conduits for fluid spread
  • Rectovesical pouch (men) / rectouterine pouch of Douglas (women): most dependent space when standing
A patient with a perforated duodenal ulcer often gets infection in the subphrenic recess on the right (subphrenic abscess). Free air on upright x-ray = perforated viscus.

Chunk 4 of 6 — The 4 prevertebral ganglia (where sympathetics synapse)

  • Celiac ganglion — at celiac trunk, T12. Receives greater splanchnic.
  • Superior mesenteric ganglion — at SMA, L1. Receives lesser splanchnic.

Predict & Check
A patient has gallbladder pain referred to the right shoulder. Which nerve carries that referred pain?
Phrenic nerve (C3–C5). The diaphragm is innervated by phrenic, so when the gallbladder irritates the diaphragm, pain is referred to the C3–C5 dermatome over the shoulder.

Chunk 6 of 6 — Sympathetic trunk + ligation effects

The sympathetic trunk runs alongside the vertebral column. In the abdomen, lumbar sympathetic ganglia send lumbar splanchnic nerves to the inferior mesenteric ganglion (for hindgut + pelvic organs).

Common ligation effects (testable):

  • Cut greater splanchnic → loss of sympathetic to foregut + suprarenal medulla
  • Cut vagus at esophageal hiatus → loss of parasymp to foregut + midgut (decreases gastric acid — historic ulcer treatment)
  • Cut pelvic splanchnics (e.g., low anterior resection) → loss of parasymp to hindgut + pelvic organs (impotence, bladder dysfunction)
  • Cut ilioinguinal n. in hernia repair → numb anterior scrotum/labia + medial thigh
